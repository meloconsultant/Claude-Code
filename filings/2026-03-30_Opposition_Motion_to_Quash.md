### I. Introduction

Plaintiffs Carmen Iris Perez and Carmelo Rivera respectfully oppose Defendant San Bernardino Municipal Water Department's motion to quash. The motion attempts to convert a curable service dispute into a dispositive jurisdictional bar, even though Plaintiffs, acting *in propria persona*, made repeated good-faith efforts to serve the City through City-controlled locations and ultimately delivered the summons and complaint to the Chief Deputy City Clerk after being redirected by City personnel. Defendant's own papers confirm that Plaintiffs personally served City offices on January 30, 2026 at 201 North E Street, yet Defendant insists that Plaintiffs can be heard only if they produced a second proof expressly listing the summons and complaint. At minimum, the record shows substantial good-faith compliance, actual notice, no prejudice, and an immediate curative path. The motion should therefore be denied, or the Court should permit amendment of proof and immediate re-service rather than granting effective dismissal of claims involving denial of essential residential water service.

### II. Statement of Facts

Plaintiff Carmelo Rivera III is the property owner of 6561 N. Ryan Lane, San Bernardino, California 92407, and the prior sole account holder of SBMWD Account No. 94660-00. Plaintiff Carmen Iris Perez is a 65-year-old tenant and occupant of the property who was never the account holder of record. Defendant San Bernardino Municipal Water Department operates as a municipal water utility and, according to defense counsel's own caption, through the City of San Bernardino. Water service to the residence was terminated on or about March 27, 2025, and the resulting delinquent balance remained in Rivera's name only.

After the shutoff, Perez sought to open water service in her own name as a new applicant. Plaintiffs' evidence shows that SBMWD repeatedly shifted its rationale for denial. The March 25 master case brief states that early contacts focused on an alleged ID suffix issue, that defense counsel later represented at the February 2, 2026 ex parte hearing that Perez had refused to pay a deposit, and that the February 3, 2026 in-person meeting ended with Deputy General Manager Robin Ohama conditioning service on payment of Rivera's prior balance. Plaintiffs contend that this final rationale reveals the actual reason for the denial.

The Court's public portal shows that this case was filed on January 14, 2026, that an ex parte application for temporary restraining order and mandatory injunctive relief was filed on January 30, 2026, that Defendant's motion to quash was filed on February 23, 2026, that Defendant filed a reply in support of that motion on March 23, 2026, and that the hearing on the motion to quash is set for March 30, 2026 at 9:00 a.m. in Department S36.

Defendant's moving papers and reply assert that a January 14, 2026 proof of service identified service at a former, closed City Hall location and that the January 30, 2026 proof of service expressly mentions only the ex parte application. Defendant therefore argues that no valid service of summons and complaint was accomplished. Yet Defendant's own motion concedes that Plaintiffs personally served the City Clerk's Office at 201 North E Street on January 30, 2026, and Plaintiffs' March 13 rebuttal brief states that Plaintiffs were directed by City staff from one listed City location to another before ultimately delivering papers to Telicia Lopez, Chief Deputy City Clerk, at 201 North E Street.

Meanwhile, the underlying merits remain serious. Plaintiffs' evidence includes an SSA benefit verification letter showing Perez's prior Philadelphia address, a February 3, 2026 meeting record log, a leak notice addressed to Carmelo Rivera, a residential lease for 6561 N. Ryan Lane, photographs documenting water hauling, medical discharge papers, and photographs documenting physical harm and weight loss. The March 25 master brief further records approximately eleven months without normal water service, APS intervention on May 22, 2025, and significant health and habitability consequences.

### III. Legal Standard

A defendant may specially appear to move to quash service of summons for lack of personal jurisdiction. Once such a motion is made, the plaintiff bears the burden to establish the facts requisite to effective service. At the same time, California trial courts retain discretion to permit correction of curable defects, amendment of proof, and prompt re-service where jurisdictional objections concern the method or proof of service rather than absence of notice or prejudice.

Service on a public entity is governed by Code of Civil Procedure section 416.50, which authorizes delivery of the summons and complaint to the clerk, secretary, president, presiding officer, or other head of the governing body. Defendant's own motion confirms that the City Clerk's Office at 201 North E Street is the operative service location for the City and that Defendant specially appeared on February 2, 2026 rather than generally appearing. The special-appearance statute, Code of Civil Procedure section 418.11, preserves Defendant's right to challenge service, but it does not convert every curable proof defect into a merits-ending result.

### IV. Argument

#### A. Plaintiffs Made Repeated Good-Faith Efforts To Serve the Proper City Official, and the Record Supports Service Through the City Clerk's Office

This is not a case in which Plaintiffs ignored service requirements or attempted to conceal the action. Defendant's own papers state that Plaintiffs personally served the City Clerk's Office at 201 North E Street on January 30, 2026. Plaintiffs' previously filed rebuttal brief goes further: it states that Plaintiffs first attempted service at the publicly listed City Clerk location, were redirected by City personnel to another City facility, were redirected again, and ultimately delivered the papers to Telicia Lopez, Chief Deputy City Clerk. That factual account, if credited, places service squarely within Code of Civil Procedure section 416.50 because the City Clerk's Office is the authorized public-entity service office and the Chief Deputy City Clerk is within the class of officials authorized to accept service for the City.

Defendant focuses on the January 14 proof of service because that form is facially problematic. But the existence of an erroneous or incomplete earlier proof does not negate a later valid service event, especially where the later service is admitted to have occurred at the City Clerk's Office. Nor does a proof form's omission necessarily disprove what was actually delivered. The key factual question is what papers were handed to the authorized City official at 201 North E Street. Plaintiffs are prepared to show through declaration that the summons and complaint were among the papers delivered after City personnel directed Plaintiffs to the proper office.

In addition, Defendant's own papers expose internal inconsistency. The moving papers describe the allegedly erroneous January 14 proof as listing service at 300 North D Street. The March 23 reply brief instead states that the defective proof listed 300 North E Street. That discrepancy does not prove service, but it undermines Defendant's effort to characterize Plaintiffs' conduct as intentional fraud rather than the sort of confusion that can occur when pro se litigants are directed among multiple City-controlled locations and then later attack is mounted from the docket image rather than from a neutral witness to the actual January 30 delivery.

#### B. Defendant's Fraud Accusation Is Unsupported, Prejudicial, and Does Not Supply a Basis To Quash Service

Defendant's motion does not merely argue technical insufficiency. It accuses Plaintiffs of attempting to perpetrate a fraud on the Court. That accusation is serious and should not be accepted on the present record. The motion's theory rests on three propositions: an earlier proof of service used a former City Hall address; no City employee accepted service at that closed location; and the January 30 proof lists only the ex parte application. But even if those propositions are true, they do not establish intentional deceit. They are equally consistent with confusion, misdescription, or imperfect completion of proof-of-service forms by self-represented litigants who were actively trying to serve the City while seeking emergency water relief.

The better reading of the record is that Plaintiffs did what pro se litigants often do: they attempted service at the address they believed corresponded to the City Clerk, followed directions from City personnel, and then served the City Clerk's Office when told where to go. Defendant can contest whether the summons and complaint were in the January 30 packet, but it has not presented evidence from the City Clerk employee who received the papers on that date. Instead, it relies on counsel's inferences from filed forms. That is insufficient to sustain a finding that Plaintiffs attempted fraud. The Court should reject the pejorative framing and treat the dispute for what it is: a correctable service-and-proof issue.

#### C. Even If the Court Finds the Present Proof Insufficient, the Proper Remedy Is To Permit Amendment of Proof and Immediate Re-Service Rather Than To Grant a Merits-Impeding Quash Without Cure

Defendant's motion challenges personal jurisdiction, not the underlying claims. The public portal confirms the case remains active, that Plaintiffs have been litigating the TRO and merits issues for weeks, and that Defendant has participated through special appearances, oppositions, declarations, and reply papers. There is no prejudice in allowing Plaintiffs to amend proof or effectuate immediate re-service on the City Clerk if the Court determines that the current filed proofs do not adequately document service of the summons and complaint.

This Court should be especially cautious here because the litigation concerns alleged denial of essential residential water service to a senior tenant and the conditioning of new service on payment of someone else's delinquent balance. Dismissal or delay on purely technical service grounds would not resolve the statutory dispute; it would merely postpone adjudication while preserving the same substantive claims. If the Court has any doubt about the January 30 service record, Plaintiffs respectfully request alternative relief allowing amendment of the proof of service, a brief continuance for filing a corrected declaration from the person who made service, or leave to immediately re-serve the summons and complaint on the City Clerk under section 416.50.

#### D. The Underlying Claims Are Substantial, and the Court Should Not Lose Sight of the Public-Interest Context

Although the present motion is jurisdictional, the context matters. Plaintiffs' evidence and the uploaded legal research show substantial statutory protections against conditioning a new tenant's water service on a prior occupant's unpaid balance. The March 25 master brief identifies Public Utilities Code sections 10009(b) and 10009.6(b) as lead theories and explains that Perez's SSA benefit verification letter showing a Philadelphia address defeats the statute's carve-out for adults who lived at the residence during the debt-accrual period. The same materials identify parallel protections under the Water Shutoff Protection Act, Health and Safety Code section 116900 et seq., including tenant protections and customer-of-record rights.

The Court's March 30 ruling should therefore preserve a path to resolution on the merits. If the Court finds service adequate, the motion should be denied outright. If the Court finds the present proof incomplete, the equitable and practical result is to allow cure, not to give Defendant a windfall based on a curable documentation dispute while the parties continue fighting over whether an elderly tenant was unlawfully denied essential utility service.

### V. Conclusion

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiffs respectfully request that the Court deny Defendant's motion to quash. In the alternative, if the Court determines the existing proof of service does not sufficiently document service of the summons and complaint, Plaintiffs request leave to amend the proof of service, permission to file a supplemental declaration from the person who made delivery, and leave to complete immediate re-service on the City Clerk under Code of Civil Procedure section 416.50 without dismissal of Plaintiffs' claims. Plaintiffs further request such other and further relief as the Court deems just and proper.

**PLAINTIFFS' SUPPLEMENTAL BRIEF RE PUBLIC UTILITIES CODE SECTIONS 10009(b), 10009.6, HEALTH AND SAFETY CODE SECTION 116900 ET SEQ., MOOTNESS, AND CCP SECTION 128.7**

### I. Purpose of Supplemental Brief

Plaintiffs submit this supplemental brief to assist the Court in evaluating the statutory framework governing Defendant's refusal to provide water service to Plaintiff Carmen Iris Perez, to address Defendant's mootness argument, and to respond to Defendant's invocation of Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7 in connection with its fraud rhetoric. This brief does not duplicate the Opposition but addresses discrete legal issues that bear on the March 30, 2026 hearing.

### II. Public Utilities Code Section 10009(b)

Public Utilities Code section 10009(b) prohibits a municipal water district from conditioning new residential service to a new customer on payment of a prior customer's delinquent balance. The statute reflects the Legislature's recognition that allowing a utility to pass prior debt obligations to a new applicant who had no prior account creates a barrier to essential services that falls disproportionately on tenants and other successor occupants.

The operative question under section 10009(b) is whether the new applicant was a customer of record during the period the debt accrued. Plaintiff Carmen Iris Perez was never an account holder of record for SBMWD Account No. 94660-00. The account was held solely by Carmelo Rivera III. Perez's SSA benefit verification letter demonstrates that she resided in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania — not at 6561 N. Ryan Lane — during the relevant period. Defendant cannot invoke any carve-out for co-residents who benefited from the service during the delinquency period because Perez was not a resident of the Property during that time.

Defendant's February 3, 2026 conditioning of service on payment of Rivera's balance is a facially unlawful act under section 10009(b) and constitutes the core statutory violation at issue in this action.

### III. Public Utilities Code Section 10009.6

Public Utilities Code section 10009.6 provides additional protections for tenants seeking to establish independent utility service. Section 10009.6(b) specifically addresses the rights of tenants to obtain service in their own names when the prior account was held by a landlord or another party, and prohibits the utility from requiring payment of the prior account holder's arrears as a condition of new tenant service.

Under section 10009.6, Perez's status as a tenant — rather than an owner-occupant or prior account holder — entitles her to establish new service without satisfying Rivera's outstanding balance. The statute is unambiguous, and SBMWD's conduct in conditioning service on Rivera's balance payment is directly contrary to its requirements.

### IV. Health and Safety Code Section 116900 et seq. (Water Shutoff Protection Act)

The Water Shutoff Protection Act, Health and Safety Code section 116900 et seq., establishes a statewide framework for residential water service termination and restoration that applies to public water systems, including municipal water departments. Among its core provisions, the Act:

- Requires written notice before service termination;
- Establishes timelines and procedures for reconnection;
- Creates specific protections for customers who are elderly, disabled, or suffer from medical conditions;
- Provides rights of appeal and dispute resolution before termination is effectuated.

Plaintiff Carmen Iris Perez is 65 years of age and has documented medical conditions. The Water Shutoff Protection Act's tenant and customer-of-record protections are directly implicated by SBMWD's sustained denial of service. The Act's protections are cumulative with those under the Public Utilities Code and support both injunctive relief and a damages claim for violations of statutory duty.

### V. Mootness Does Not Extinguish Plaintiffs' Claims

Defendant may argue, or has argued, that changes in the service status render some or all of Plaintiffs' claims moot. That argument fails for several independent reasons.

**First**, if water service has been restored or conditionally provided during the pendency of this litigation, that change was made in the shadow of this lawsuit. Voluntary cessation of unlawful conduct does not moot a claim unless the defendant can demonstrate with certainty that the conduct will not recur. Defendant has not made and cannot make that showing.

**Second**, Plaintiffs' damages claims — for the approximately eleven months of denial of service, documented health consequences, costs of water hauling, and other harm — survive any change in current service status. Mootness doctrine does not extinguish accrued damages claims.

**Third**, a declaratory judgment establishing that SBMWD's practice of conditioning new-tenant service on prior-account-holder balances violates the Public Utilities Code and Health and Safety Code remains a live controversy of public importance. The challenged practice, if not formally repudiated, will continue to affect other similarly situated tenants.

### VI. CCP Section 128.7 and Defendant's Fraud Rhetoric

Defendant's motion to quash characterizes Plaintiffs' service conduct as an attempt to perpetrate a fraud on the Court. Defendant has not filed a formal motion for sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7, but its papers make serious accusations that require a direct response.

Section 128.7 requires that an attorney or unrepresented party certify that any paper filed with the court is not presented for an improper purpose, is warranted by existing law or a nonfrivolous argument for modification of law, and that the factual contentions have or are likely to have evidentiary support. If Defendant intends to press sanctions-related relief, it must follow the safe-harbor procedures of section 128.7(c)(1) before filing such a motion with the Court.

More fundamentally, the record does not support the fraud characterization. As set forth in the Opposition, any errors in the proofs of service filed by Plaintiffs are consistent with the confusion that self-represented litigants encounter when navigating unfamiliar legal procedures and being redirected among multiple government offices. The operative question is whether service was actually accomplished — a factual dispute that should be resolved on evidence, not rhetoric.

Plaintiffs do not seek sanctions at this juncture. They request that the Court disregard the pejorative framing and decide the service issue on the factual and legal merits.

### VII. Conclusion

The statutory framework under Public Utilities Code sections 10009(b) and 10009.6 and Health and Safety Code section 116900 et seq. provides substantial support for Plaintiffs' claims on the merits. Mootness does not bar those claims. Defendant's fraud rhetoric exceeds what the record supports and should not influence the Court's resolution of the curable service dispute. Plaintiffs respectfully request that the Court deny the motion to quash and permit this action to proceed to resolution on its merits.
